# Gurudwara & Anand Karaj — Guest Guide

Full content for the FAQ page, attire page, and Anand Karaj event description. Intended for non-Sikh guests and anyone attending a Gurudwara for the first time.

## Before You Arrive

### What to Wear

**The most important rule: cover your head.** This is a sign of respect before the Guru Granth Sahib (the Sikh holy scripture), and applies to everyone inside the Gurudwara — regardless of faith, gender, or background.

- **Head covering:** Scarves, bandanas, and patkas (Sikh head coverings) will be available at the entrance. You are also welcome to bring your own. Hair does not need to be fully covered — a scarf loosely draped over the top of your head is perfectly appropriate.
- **Modest dress:** Shoulders and knees should be covered. Avoid sleeveless tops, shorts, or anything form-fitting.
- **Socks:** You will remove your shoes at the entrance — wear socks or plan to go barefoot inside. The floors are clean but cool.
- **No leather:** Traditionally, leather items are not brought into the Darbar Hall as a sign of respect for all living beings.
- **Colors:** Bright, festive colors are warmly welcomed. There is no all-white or all-black dress requirement.

- The Gurudwara is approximately 20–30 minutes from the hotel.
- Parking is available on-site if you prefer to drive.

## Arriving at the Gurudwara

1. **Remove your shoes** at the entrance. Shoe racks or a designated area are provided. Keep your socks on — the floors of a Gurudwara are kept very clean.
2. **Cover your head** before entering. Coverings are available at the door if you need one.
3. **Wash your hands** at the hand-washing station near the entrance — this is customary before entering.
4. **Enter the Darbar Hall** (main hall). As you approach the Guru Granth Sahib (the holy book, installed at the front on a raised platform called the Palki), you will see people bow — this is called **Matha Tekna**.

### Matha Tekna — Bowing Before the Guru Granth Sahib

You will see Sikhs approach the Guru Granth Sahib, kneel, and touch their forehead to the ground before sitting. You are welcome but never required to do this. If you choose to bow, simply walk up, kneel, touch your forehead to the mat in front of the Palki, and then sit down to the side. If you prefer not to bow, a respectful nod as you enter is perfectly fine.

### Sitting in the Darbar Hall

- Everyone sits on the floor. Cushions or floor mats are provided.
- Men and women typically sit on opposite sides of the hall, though this varies by Gurudwara. Follow the lead of others.
- **Do not point your feet toward the Guru Granth Sahib.** Sit cross-legged or with your feet tucked to the side. This is a sign of respect.
- No eating, drinking, or chewing gum inside the Darbar Hall.
- Keep your voice low. The hall is a place of reverence and active worship.

## The Anand Karaj Ceremony

### What It Means

*Anand Karaj* means "blissful union." It is a Sikh wedding ceremony that takes place in the presence of the Guru Granth Sahib. The couple's union is considered spiritual as much as worldly — they are joined not just to each other but to Waheguru (God) and the Sikh community.

### The Ceremony at a Glance

**Milni (9:00 AM)**
The two families formally meet and greet each other in the courtyard or entrance area. Garlands may be exchanged, gifts given, and prayers recited. This is a joyful, somewhat informal gathering — photographs welcome outside.

**Anand Karaj (10:00 AM)**
Devotional music performed live by ragis (musicians) using harmonium and tabla. The Gurbani (scripture) is sung in Punjabi. You don't need to understand the words to feel the beauty — let it wash over you. The congregation typically sways gently or sits quietly in meditation.

**Ardas (Prayer)**
A communal standing prayer recited before the ceremony begins. Everyone in the hall stands. You are welcome to stand respectfully even if you don't know the words.

**The Four Lavan (Wedding Rounds)**
The heart of the ceremony. The couple walks around the Guru Granth Sahib four times — called the *lavan* — while the ragis sing four corresponding hymns from the Guru Granth Sahib. Each round represents a stage of the spiritual journey:

| Lavan | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| First | Departure from worldly attachments; beginning of the divine path |
| Second | The union of the mind with the Divine; the couple moves toward God together |
| Third | Detachment from desire; love and longing for the Divine fills the heart |
| Fourth | Complete union — the soul merges with God; the marriage is sealed |

The groom leads, the bride follows, holding the end of his scarf (*palla*). After each round the couple bows before the Guru Granth Sahib.

**Anand Sahib**
The congregation sings the *Anand Sahib* ("Song of Bliss"), a hymn of joy celebrating the union. This marks the formal conclusion of the ceremony.

**Ardas & Hukamnama**
A closing prayer is recited, then a *Hukamnama* — a random verse from the Guru Granth Sahib read aloud as divine guidance for the couple's new life together.

**Karah Parshad**
Sacred sweet (a warm semolina pudding) is distributed to all present after the ceremony. Accepting it is a blessing — hold out both hands cupped together to receive it. You are welcome to eat it or simply hold it as a gesture of respect.

## Langar (12:00 PM)

After the ceremony, all guests — regardless of faith, background, or status — are invited to Langar, the free community meal. This is a central Sikh tradition: everyone sits together on the floor (or at tables, depending on the setup) as equals and shares a vegetarian meal. It is an honor and a pleasure to participate.

- **The food is always vegetarian** (and often vegan).
- Expect simple, nourishing Punjabi food: dal, sabzi, roti, kheer.
- You may be served by volunteers (*sevadars*) — this is considered a form of seva (selfless service).
- Sit where you like. There is no hierarchy in Langar.

---

## Dohli (1:30 PM)

The *Dohli* is the bride's departure from her family's side. It is an emotional ceremony — the bride formally leaves her family home (symbolically) to begin her life with her husband's family. Expect tears, songs, and hugs. It is customary to give the bride words of blessing and love. Photography is generally welcome but read the moment — it can be deeply personal.

---

## Photography Guidelines

- **Outside the Gurudwara:** Photography of the Milni, Barat arrival, and courtyard moments is generally fine.
- **Inside the Darbar Hall:** Many Gurudwaras restrict photography during active worship. **Follow the couple's instructions and the lead of the family.** A wedding program or announcement will clarify. When in doubt, put your phone down and simply be present — the ceremony is worth experiencing fully.
- **Flash photography** is typically not permitted inside.
- **Dohli:** Sensitive moment — be respectful and don't photograph grieving family members without permission.

## For Non-Sikh Guests — Common Questions

**Do I need to be Sikh to attend?**
Absolutely not. Gurudwaras are open to all people of all faiths and backgrounds. You will be welcomed warmly.

**Do I need to participate in the religious elements?**
No. You are a guest. Sit respectfully, observe, and participate only in what feels comfortable. No one will pressure you to bow, pray, or recite anything.

**Is there anything I should not do?**
- Do not bring alcohol or tobacco onto the Gurudwara grounds.
- Do not bring meat products inside.
- Do not turn your back on the Guru Granth Sahib when leaving the Darbar Hall — back out slowly or turn when you're further from the platform.
- Keep your voice low inside.

**What if I don't want to receive Karah Parshad?**
Simply hold your hands out respectfully and accept it — you can hold it without eating it. Declining entirely may be seen as slightly rude, so accepting graciously (even if you don't eat it) is appreciated.

**Will there be English?**
The ceremony is conducted in Gurbani (Punjabi scripture). A printed program with English translations and explanations will be provided. The family may also offer brief explanations during the ceremony.

**How long does the ceremony take?**
The Anand Karaj itself is approximately 45–60 minutes. With Milni, chai, and Langar, plan for the full morning — 8:30 AM through 1:30 PM.
